The Internet

The internet is a global network of interconnected computers that allows billions of people to communicate, share information, and access resources from anywhere in the world. It is one of the most transformative inventions in human history.

Origins

Born out of Cold War fears, the internet began as a government project to create a communication network that could survive a nuclear attack. Early visionaries imagined a world where computers could talk to each other across vast distances — and they made it happen.

ARPANET

In 1969, the Advanced Research Projects Agency Network (ARPANET) became the first packet-switched network. It connected four universities — UCLA, Stanford, UC Santa Barbara, and the University of Utah — laying the foundation for everything that followed.

The First Message

The very first message ever sent over ARPANET was supposed to be "LOGIN", but the system crashed after just two letters. The first transmission in internet history was simply: "LO". A humble beginning for a world-changing technology.

Key Figures

Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn invented TCP/IP, the protocol that became the language of the internet. J.C.R. Licklider first described the concept of a "Galactic Network" of computers in 1962, nearly a decade before ARPANET existed.

Did You Know?

For its first two decades, the internet was almost exclusively used by military personnel, government agencies, and university researchers. The general public had no idea it existed.

The World Wide Web

In 1991, British scientist Tim Berners-Lee introduced the World Wide Web — a system of linked documents accessible via the internet. It is important to note: the web is not the internet. The internet is the infrastructure; the web is just one of many services that runs on top of it.

Browsers and HTML

The first widely used web browser, Mosaic, launched in 1993. Netscape followed shortly after and dominated the early web. These browsers rendered HTML pages and made the web accessible to everyday users for the first time, sparking an explosion of online content.

The Dot-Com Boom

The late 1990s saw a frenzy of investment in internet companies. Hundreds of startups launched overnight, many with no clear business model. The bubble burst in 2000, but the companies that survived — Amazon, Google — went on to reshape the world.

Notable Survivors

Yahoo was once the front page of the internet. Amazon started as an online bookstore. Google launched in 1998 and quickly became the definitive way to find anything online. These companies outlasted the crash and became some of the most valuable in history.

Fun Fact

Google was originally called "BackRub" — named after its technique of analyzing back-links to rank web pages. Founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin renamed it to "Google", a play on "googol", the number 1 followed by 100 zeros.

The Modern Web

Today the internet is woven into nearly every aspect of daily life. Social media, video streaming, cloud computing, and mobile connectivity have transformed how we work, communicate, shop, and entertain ourselves.

Web 2.0

Around 2004, the web shifted from static pages to dynamic, user-generated content. Blogs, YouTube, Wikipedia, and eventually Facebook and Twitter turned ordinary users into creators and contributors. The read-only web became a read-write web.

Smartphones Change Everything

The launch of the iPhone in 2007 put the internet in everyone's pocket. Mobile internet usage eventually surpassed desktop, and apps replaced browsers as the primary way many people access online services. The world was never the same.

Today's Internet

Over 5 billion people are connected to the internet today. We stream movies, attend virtual meetings, control smart home devices, and interact with AI assistants — all through the same network that sent two letters in 1969.

What's Next?

The next evolution may bring Web3 and decentralized networks, spatial computing and augmented reality, and AI-driven experiences that blur the line between the physical and digital world. The internet's story is far from over.
