The Early Internet

A Journey Through the 1990s and 2000s

Before social media, before smartphones, before streaming — there was the dial-up modem, the screeching sound of connection, and the wild frontier of the early web. This page is a tribute to that era.

The Sound of the Internet

If you were there, you remember it: the 56k modem shrieking to life, the agonizing wait for a single image to load line by line, and the dreaded words — "Someone picked up the phone." Connection lost.

The Browser Wars

Netscape Navigator ruled the early web until Microsoft bundled Internet Explorer with Windows and ignited one of the fiercest battles in tech history. By 2004, IE had over 90% market share — but it wouldn't last.

The Rise of Search

Before Google, people used AltaVista, Lycos, and Ask Jeeves to find things online. Then, in 1998, two Stanford students launched a search engine with a funny name and changed everything.

Pop-ups and Wild West Design

Early websites were gloriously chaotic — tiling backgrounds, blinking text, visitor counters, and pop-up ads everywhere. Design had no rules. Everything was possible. Nothing was tasteful.

Timeline of Key Moments

Ordered Timeline

  1. 1991 — Tim Berners-Lee launches the World Wide Web
  2. 1993 — Mosaic browser makes the web visual and accessible
  3. 1994 — Netscape Navigator released; Yahoo! founded
  4. 1995 — Amazon and eBay launch; JavaScript invented
  5. 1998 — Google founded; the dot-com frenzy peaks
  6. 2000 — Dot-com bubble bursts; millions of sites disappear overnight
  7. 2001 — Wikipedia launches; broadband starts replacing dial-up
  8. 2003 — MySpace launches; blogs explode in popularity
  9. 2004 — Facebook founded; Firefox challenges Internet Explorer
  10. 2005 — YouTube launches; Web 2.0 era begins
